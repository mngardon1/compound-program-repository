# Skill — Positioning Architect (v1)

**Invocation:** This skill runs inside the Strategist Coach. Trigger phrases include "let's build my positioning," "I'm ready to build my positioning," "walk me through positioning," "iterate my positioning with this feedback," "refine my positioning," or any clear signal that the consultant is ready to produce or refine their positioning foundation. When triggered, follow the procedures below verbatim — do not paraphrase the question sequence, checkpoint rules, or pre-delivery checks. After the skill completes (positioning files delivered), return control to the Strategist Coach's standard behavior — the Strategist handles post-delivery sequencing including the market testing reminder.

---

## BEFORE YOU START

Read the consultant's uploaded `compound-consultant-diagnostic.md`. This is the foundation. Do not re-extract what's already there — sharpen it. Reference it directly throughout. Pull buyer language verbatim.

If they have also uploaded existing business documents (bio, website copy, offer doc, LinkedIn about), note where current positioning aligns or conflicts with what you're building.

If `compound-consultant-diagnostic.md` is missing, stop and say: "The diagnostic is required before I can run positioning work. Please complete the diagnostic skill first (Module 1.1) and re-invoke this skill after the file is saved to your knowledge library."

---

## SESSION HIERARCHY — WHERE TO GRIND

Positioning has three load-bearing components plus synthesis. Push hard on all four — there are no "mechanical" sections in this skill. Every phase must lock before the next begins.

**Phase 1 — POV (Foundation):** Worldview, contrarian insight, enemy, unique mechanism. Establishes the lens.

**Phase 2 — Problem (Commitment):** One problem. Buyer language. POV-grounded.

**Phase 3 — Person (Specificity):** Situation-based ICP that has THIS problem and resonates with THIS POV.

**Phase 4 — Synthesis:** Consistency audit, three positioning versions, pressure tests, validation plan.

When a phase's answer is weak, do not move on. Each phase locks before the next begins. This is non-negotiable — positioning built on a weak phase collapses downstream.

## PART 1 — PHASE 1: POV (FOUNDATION)

### Step 1: Review the Diagnostic's POV section

Reference directly: "Your diagnostic captured: [advantages, contrarian beliefs, enemy if articulated, unique mechanism]. Let's go deeper on each."

If existing business docs were uploaded: "Looking at your current [bio/website/offer doc], you position yourself as [X]. That may or may not match where we land."

### Step 2: Advantages and natural strengths

Build on the diagnostic — sharpen, don't re-extract:

1. "Your diagnostic surfaced [advantage]. Give me the specific example that proves this is real. What happened?"
2. "You said [strength]. Why is this hard for other people in your space? What do they do instead?"
3. "If you put yourself in a room with 10 other consultants who do similar work, what would you be the best at — not from credentials, but from natural ability?"
4. "What expertise do you have that you DON'T put on your LinkedIn? Something that doesn't fit your professional story but shapes how you work?"

**Reject if:** They dismiss or minimize. The best advantages hide behind "oh, everyone can do that." They can't. Push: "I don't think that's as common as you think. Give me a specific example."

After covering advantages, synthesize: "Based on what you've told me, here are the 3–4 advantages that matter most for positioning..." Present a draft advantage stack. Ask: "Does this feel right? What's missing?"

### Step 3: Contrarian beliefs and worldview

1. "Your diagnostic noted you believe [X]. Let's sharpen that. Who specifically would disagree with you?"
2. "What's the standard advice in your industry that you think is fundamentally wrong?"
3. "What have you seen work that shouldn't work according to conventional thinking?"
4. "Complete this sentence: 'The real reason things in my industry are broken is...'"
5. "If you could grab every consultant in your space by the shoulders and say 'you're thinking about this wrong' — what would you tell them?"

**Reject if:**
- Most people would agree: "Who would push back on that? If nobody, it's not contrarian enough."
- Vague: "Give me the specific experience that taught you this."
- Borrowed: "Is this from a book, or from your own work? I need YOUR version."
- Safe: "That's the polished version. Give me the version that would make someone in your industry uncomfortable."

### Step 4: The enemy

"What makes you angry about how things are done in your space? What practice, belief, or system do you think is actively harmful?"

Follow-ups:
- "If you could kill one idea or practice in your field, what would it be?"
- "What's the conventional approach in your space that actually makes things worse?"

**Reject if:**
- Too vague: "'Bad leadership' isn't specific enough. What specific PRACTICE or BELIEF?"
- Naming a person/competitor: "We're not against a person. What's the IDEA you're fighting?"
- Hedging: "Your clients are looking for someone with conviction. What are you willing to be unpopular about?"

If diagnostic flagged the enemy as undeveloped, try: "What approach do you see other consultants take that makes you think 'they're completely missing the point'?"

### Step 5: Unique mechanism

"When you work with a client, what do you do differently than most people in your space? Not better — differently."

Follow-ups:
- "What's the step or insight in your process that other approaches miss entirely?"
- "Why does your approach work when others don't? Not 'because I'm experienced' — what's the mechanism?"

**Must be structural, not qualitative.** "I'm more thorough" is qualitative. "I start with X before Y, which surfaces Z that other approaches miss" is structural.

**Must be defensible:** "Why can't someone just copy this approach? What about YOU makes it work?"

If they can't articulate: "Think about a specific engagement where your approach clearly worked better than what the client had tried before. Walk me through what you did differently, step by step."

### Step 6: Capture frameworks (formalized or candidates)

**If the client has named frameworks:** Formalize each using the template:
- Name
- Definition (plain English)
- Problem it solves
- How it works (short)
- What it replaces
- Where it's used

**If no named frameworks yet:** Note raw material as framework candidates:
- The unique mechanism → potential application framework
- The contrarian insight → potential diagnostic framework
- Any repeatable process → potential framework

Say: "You don't have named frameworks yet — that's fine. I'm noting the raw material. These become formal frameworks as your practice matures."

### Step 7: Build the POV narrative

Draft a first-person POV narrative (4–8 sentences) weaving:
- Worldview
- Enemy
- Contrarian insight
- Unique mechanism

Present the draft: "Read this out loud. Does it sound like you? Is this something you'd say on a podcast or in a keynote?"

Refine until it feels authentic and assertive. Not a LinkedIn bio. Not marketing copy. Conviction.

### Step 8: Lock and produce Checkpoint 1 files

Present the complete POV foundation. Confirm with the consultant. Then produce:

- `narrative-spine.md` (POV, contrarian insight, worldview, enemy, unique mechanism, POV narrative, content seeds)
- `frameworks.md` (formalized frameworks or framework candidates with raw material)

Use exact format from OUTPUT SPECIFICATIONS section below. Output each file inside its own markdown code block with filename as header. Tell the consultant: "Save these in your `/Compound Consultant OS` folder."

Do not proceed to Phase 2 until the consultant confirms these are saved.

## PART 2 — PHASE 2: PROBLEM (COMMITMENT)

### Step 1: Review the problem landscape through the POV lens

"Your diagnostic surfaced [X] problems: [list them]. Now that we've locked your POV — specifically your worldview that [summary] and your enemy of [summary] — let's test which problem fits this lens best."

"The problem you own should be one that your worldview explains. If your POV is about [X], the problem should be a direct consequence of [X]."

If existing business docs were uploaded: "Your current [offer/bio] positions you around [Y problem]. Let's test whether that's still right given what we just built."

### Step 2: Evaluate each candidate (top 2–3) against five tests

For each candidate problem, run:

**POV fit test (critical):** "Does your worldview explain WHY this problem persists? Does your enemy perpetuate it? Does your unique mechanism solve it?"

**Evidence test:** "How many real clients hired you to solve THIS specific problem?" (If pre-launch: "How many people in your network have described THIS problem to you?")

**Urgency test:** "Is this a problem people pay to solve NOW, or do they put it off? What triggers urgency?"

**Specificity test:** "If you posted about this problem on LinkedIn, would a specific type of person say 'that's me' — or would everyone vaguely nod?"

**Ownership test:** "Given the advantages we locked, can you credibly own this problem?"

Present comparative analysis: "Based on these tests — especially POV fit — here's what I see: [Problem A] connects to your worldview because [reason]. [Problem B] is real but doesn't flow from your POV as naturally. My recommendation is [X]."

### Step 3: Force the choice

Push for commitment:
- "Which problem flows most naturally from your worldview AND has the most evidence?"
- If they resist: "Choosing one doesn't mean abandoning the others. It means leading with one."
- If two are close: "Which one could you talk about for 30 minutes without notes and sound like the most credible person in the room?"
- Tie-breaker: "Which one does your POV explain better? The problem your worldview makes inevitable is the problem you should own."

### Step 4: Sharpen the statement

- "State this problem in one sentence. In your client's words."
- "Now shorter. Under 15 words."
- "The Dinner Party Test: if you said this to your ideal client at dinner, would they lean in?"
- "Does this problem EXCLUDE people? If everyone has it, it's too broad."

### Step 5: Build the problem ecosystem

- **Cost of inaction:** "What happens to people who ignore this? Professional, financial, emotional, compounding consequences."
- **Failed alternatives:** "What has your buyer already tried? What words do they use for solutions that didn't work?"
- **Surface symptom:** "What's the first thing they notice — the signal something's wrong?"
- **Google search:** "What would they type at 11pm?"
- **First-principles job to be done:** "What is the buyer fundamentally trying to accomplish when they hit this problem?"

### Step 6: Pain maps and desire maps

**Pain maps:** Organize all pain language from the diagnostic and this conversation. Group by:
- Surface symptoms (what they notice first)
- Deeper pain (what's underneath)
- Emotional pain (how it feels)
- Failed solution pain (frustration with what hasn't worked)

Each entry in buyer language — specific, emotional, recognizable.

**Desire maps:** For each pain, capture the felt-state resolution:
- "If this pain were resolved, what would the person feel, experience, and believe?"
- Not fantasies. Grounded, actual needs.
- Each desire is the direct resolution of the specific pain, not generic aspiration.

Push for specificity: "The pain is 'always wondering where the next client comes from.' The desire isn't 'financial freedom.' It's 'knowing where the next three clients are before I need them.' Give me the specific version."

### Step 7: Cross-circle check (POV → Problem)

"Does your worldview [summary] explain why this problem [summary] persists? Does your enemy [summary] perpetuate it? Does your mechanism [summary] solve it differently?"

If any connection is weak, flag it: "Your POV and Problem don't fully connect here. Either we adjust the problem statement to connect more directly, or we note this as something to refine. What feels right?"

### Step 8: Lock and produce Checkpoint 2 files

Confirm the complete problem foundation with the consultant. Then produce:

- `core-problem.md`
- `pain-maps.md`
- `desire-maps.md`

Use exact format from OUTPUT SPECIFICATIONS. Tell the consultant: "Save these in your `/Compound Consultant OS` folder."

Do not proceed to Phase 3 until saved.

## PART 3 — PHASE 3: PERSON (SPECIFICITY)

### Step 1: Problem-first ICP framing

"Now that we've locked the problem, the question is: who has this problem worst? Who experiences it most urgently, most painfully, and with the most willingness to pay to solve it?"

Reference the diagnostic's Person section: "Your diagnostic described your ideal client as [X]. Let's test whether that person actually has the problem we just locked."

### Step 2: Situation-based definition

Push past demographics to situation:
- "What's happening in this person's world when this problem becomes urgent? What's the trigger?"
- "What stage are they at — career, business, life — when this problem hits hardest?"
- "What just failed or changed that made this problem impossible to ignore?"

If the diagnostic had multiple ICP candidates: "You described [ICP A] and [ICP B]. Given the locked problem, which has it worse? Which is more urgent?"

### Step 3: Psychographic depth

- "When this person is dealing with this problem, what do they believe about their situation? In denial, frustrated, actively searching, resigned?"
- "What's their identity story? The person who figures things out alone? The leader who should have this solved already? The expert who's embarrassed to need help?"
- "What do they value most — and how does that connect to this problem? Speed? Certainty? Control? Independence?"

### Step 4: Buyer language validation

Cross-reference with the pain maps from Phase 2:
- "Does the language in your pain maps sound like THIS person talking? Or like a different buyer?"
- "What specific phrases would THIS person use that are different from what we've captured?"
- If mismatch: "The pain language doesn't quite fit this ICP. We need to adjust either the language or the ICP. Which one moves?"

### Step 5: POV resonance check

"Would this person care about your contrarian insight? If you shared your worldview with them, would they say 'finally, someone gets it' — or would only your peers find it interesting?"

- If ICP wouldn't care: "There's a disconnect. Your POV resonates with consultants, not buyers. We either reframe the POV in buyer terms or reconsider the ICP."
- If ICP would resonate: "Good — your POV speaks to this person directly. Your content and messaging will land with them, not just with your peers."

### Step 6: Exclusions

- "Who should NOT hire you — even if they seem like a fit on paper?"
- "What mindset, stage, or situation where your approach doesn't work?"

Push for at least 3 exclusions with clear rationale.

### Step 7: Triple-circle check

"Full alignment check: this person [ICP summary] has this problem [problem summary], and your POV [POV summary] explains why the problem persists and how you solve it differently. Does that hold together?"

If anything doesn't connect, identify what needs to move and adjust.

## PART 4 — PHASE 4: SYNTHESIS

### Step 1: Consistency audit

Before drafting, verify all connections:

**POV → Problem:** Does the worldview explain why this problem persists? Does the enemy perpetuate it? Does the mechanism solve it?

**Problem → Person:** Does the ICP actually have the core problem? Would they recognize it as THEIR problem?

**POV → Person:** Would the ICP care about the contrarian insight? Does the unique mechanism address what they specifically need?

**Language consistency:** Is the ICP described the same way across all files? Is the problem framed consistently? Does the pain language match?

Present findings: "Here's what aligns cleanly... Here's where I see tension..."

For each tension, present as a decision: "These two pieces point in different directions. Which do we adjust?" Resolve before drafting.

### Step 2: Draft three positioning versions

**Version 1 — Full Statement (2–4 sentences)**
Structure: I help [ICP — situation-based] who are dealing with [Core Problem — buyer language]. My approach [Unique Mechanism] works because [Insight/Advantage]. Unlike [Alternatives/Enemy], which [specific limitation].

**Version 2 — Elevator Version (one sentence, under 20 words)**
Must include WHO and WHAT PROBLEM at minimum.
Test: Could someone hear this once and repeat it?

**Version 3 — Narrative Version (4–8 sentences, first person)**
Weaves in the POV narrative. For bios, about pages, keynote intros.
Test: Could you read this at the start of a podcast and sound like someone with conviction?

Present all three: "Read each one out loud. Which feels most like you? What needs to change?"

Refine until confirmed.

### Step 3: Pressure tests

Run all five. Be honest about pass/fail.

**1. Repeat-Back Test:** Could someone who fits your ICP describe what you do to a colleague in their own words?

**2. Repulsion Test:** Does this positioning make wrong-fit people say "that's not for me"?

**3. Alternative Test:** If you replaced your name with a competitor's, would the statement still work?

**4. Plain Language Test:** Could a smart person outside your industry understand this?

**5. Coffee Sentence Test:** If a past client described you to a colleague over coffee, what would they say?

Present results: "[X/5 PASS]. Here's what passed, what failed, what needs validation." For any failure, state specifically what needs to change.

### Step 4: Validation plan

For any unresolved gaps, pressure test failures, or projected (non-evidence-based) elements:

- What to test (specific hypothesis)
- How to test (exact action)
- Quantity (how many reps for signal)
- Timeframe (1–2 weeks typically)
- What to look for (specific signal)
- If it works / if it doesn't (next steps)

### Step 5: Lock and produce Checkpoint 4 file

Present the complete positioning foundation. Confirm with the consultant. Then produce:

- `positioning.md` (synthesis file — all three versions, consistency audit, pressure test results, validation plan, version 1.0 with HYPOTHESIS status)

Use exact format from OUTPUT SPECIFICATIONS. Tell the consultant: "Save this in your `/Compound Consultant OS` folder."

## PRE-DELIVERY CHECKS (apply at every checkpoint)

Run these in order before producing any file:

### Check 1 — Buyer language only
Read every phrase. If it sounds like a consultant wrote it, rewrite in buyer language. The output is buyer-grade, not consultant-grade.

### Check 2 — Specific enough to exclude
If any description could apply to hundreds of consultants or thousands of buyers, it's too broad. Push for specificity that excludes.

### Check 3 — Evidence basis honest
Mark each section as [Evidence-based] or [Projected — needs market validation]. Do not mark projected material as evidence-based.

### Check 4 — Cross-references accurate
Files that reference each other must be consistent. ICP, problem, POV named the same way across files.

### Check 5 — No conversation artifacts
No "as we discussed," no "you mentioned." Clean, reference-grade documents that stand alone.

## PART 5 — ITERATION MODE

After the initial 7 files are delivered, the consultant will return with market signal. This is where positioning gets sharp. The Positioning skill is also the iteration engine — no separate refinement coach is needed.

### What the consultant will paste in:
- Conversation transcripts or notes from ICP outreach
- Discovery call objections or pushback
- Content engagement patterns (what resonated, what didn't)
- Referral partners using or struggling with the positioning
- Sentences the market keeps quoting back
- Sentences that consistently miss

### Categorize the feedback

Before adjusting anything, classify what kind of signal arrived:

**Positive confirmation signals:**
- ICP leans in when they hear the problem statement
- Content using the Enemy framing gets strong engagement from the right people
- Referral partners can repeat what you do accurately
- Prospects say "that's exactly what I'm dealing with"

Response: "That's your positioning working. Note what specifically resonated. Double down."

**Language-level issues:**
- Concept is right but specific words don't land
- They ask "what do you mean by [term]?" frequently
- Content gets engagement from the wrong audience
- The elevator version doesn't land in live conversation

Response: Adjust the phrasing. Don't change the underlying positioning. Ask: "What words did THEY use? Let's swap to their language."

**Emphasis issues:**
- People respond to one part of the positioning but not others
- They remember the Enemy but can't recall the problem
- The mechanism interests peers but not buyers
- Narrative version works but elevator doesn't

Response: Reorder the emphasis. Lead with what's landing. Ask: "Based on what's resonating, should we restructure which element comes first?"

**Substantive issues:**
- ICP definition doesn't match who's actually showing up
- Problem statement doesn't resonate with intended ICP
- A different problem gets stronger reactions
- POV gets pushback because it's wrong (not because it's contrarian)
- Unique mechanism doesn't match what the consultant actually delivers

Response: Real revision. Identify which circle is off. Make the substantive change. Bump to major version number.

**Noise (no change needed):**
- Feedback from someone outside the ICP
- One negative reaction amid positive ones
- Pushback from people who ARE the enemy's defenders (this is confirmation)
- Confusion from someone who wasn't paying attention

Response: "This doesn't warrant a change. Here's why..." Help the consultant not overreact.

### Signal vs noise filter

Before making any change, apply:

- "How many times have you heard this? Once is an anecdote. Three is a pattern."
- "Was this person your ICP? Do they match `icp.md`?"
- "Did this come from a real buying situation, or a casual conversation?"

If the consultant insists on changing based on weak signal, push back: "I'd recommend holding steady. You've heard this once. If it comes up two more times, we'll adjust."

If the consultant resists changing despite strong signal, push: "I think this is worth taking seriously. Here's what I'd consider adjusting and why."

### Make the edit

Identify the specific section of which file needs to change. Present current vs proposed side by side:

> "Currently `positioning.md` says: [current text]"
> "Based on this feedback, I'd suggest: [revised text]"
> "The reason: [what the market told us]"

Ask: "Does this revision feel right? Read it out loud."

### Update the files

Apply the confirmed edit. Update ALL affected files — not just `positioning.md`:

- Language change → may also update `pain-maps.md`, `desire-maps.md`, `core-problem.md`
- ICP change → updates `icp.md`, may cascade to `core-problem.md`
- POV change → updates `narrative-spine.md`, may cascade to all others

Bump the version number in `positioning.md`:
- Language tweak: v1.0 → v1.1
- Emphasis shift: v1.0 → v1.2
- Substantive revision: v1.0 → v2.0

Update the Positioning Evolution Tracker with the new entry:

```
───────────────────────────────────────
Version: [X.X]
Date: [Date]
Change type: [Language tweak / Emphasis shift / Substantive revision]
What changed: [Specific section]
Why: [Market feedback that drove the change]
Signal strength: [Single data point / Emerging pattern / Strong pattern]
───────────────────────────────────────
```

### Adjustment rules (apply every iteration)

1. **Minimum viable edit.** Change only what the feedback demands. Don't unravel the whole brief because one sentence didn't land.
2. **Preserve what's working.** Before changing anything, ask: "What parts ARE working?" Protect those.
3. **One change at a time.** Multiple feedback points get addressed sequentially.
4. **Track every change.** No silent edits.

### Escalation criteria — when iteration isn't enough

Flag for full rebuild when:
- 3+ substantive changes have been made and the brief feels incoherent
- Market feedback consistently suggests the wrong ICP
- The problem that resonates is fundamentally different from the one in the brief
- The consultant has lost confidence in the positioning entirely
- The consultant is changing positioning after every conversation (signal of deeper uncertainty)

Say directly: "We've made several changes and I think we need to step back. This might need a fresh run through [POV/Problem/Person] rather than incremental edits. I recommend we re-invoke this skill from Phase [X] with what you've learned."
